Finance & Food

There is a reason to believe that a significant role was played in the 2007-2008 food price crisis by the entry into markets for derivatives based on food commodities of large, powerful institutional investors such as hedge funds, pension funds and investment banks, all of which are generally unconcerned with agricultural market fundamentals. Such entry was made possible because of massive deregulation in important commodity derivatives markets beginning in 2000 with the adoption in the United States of the Commodity Futures Modernization Act. Some argue that fundamental reform of the entire global financial sector is urgently required in order to avert another food price crisis. This is also a topic that is high on the current agenda of the G20, under French presidency. This seminar will explore the meaning of speculation on the derivatives markets of agricultural commodities, and the relationships between the derivatives markets and the physical markets; the ways unhealthy speculation can be tackled; and the potential role of the international community in addressing the issue.

  • Look at the meaning of speculation on the derivatives market of agricultural commodities
    •          how did agribusinesses facilitate this process?
             what was the role of agribusinesses in speculation and creation of derivatives market?
  • Relationships between derivatives markets and physical markets
    •           trace the impact of this from abstract economy to local economies around the globe e.g. uprisings in 2008 food crisis
  • How to tackle speculation and regulate the market
  • What are the regulations around speculation and financialization in food commodity market
  • Relationship between trade flows and international trade agreements
  • Timeline about finance and deregulation of economy

Types of Corporations

Info

Most agribusiness corporations operate across world borders in many countries. Its operations are diverse and involve extensive capital and infrastructure to maintain its business and growth. Oftentimes, these corporations can control markets and prices, thus giving them immense power within the food system. This section focuses on those corporations that are involved in the global food system and visually explains its structure and role in this system.

Types of Corporations

We have identified four sub-categories of food corporations as follows:

  • Primary: a primary corporation produces and distributes raw materials such as seeds, grain, or meat for the global food system e.g. Cargill and Monsanto
  • Intermediary: an intermediary corporation produces various food products such as snacks and other packaged/processed food and drinks found in the supermarket e.g. Nestle and PepsiCo
  • Retail: a retail corporation sells food products in commercial, retail spaces e.g. Carrefour and Walmart
  • Service: a service corporation produces food on a mass scale in “fast food” chain restaurants e.g. McDonalds and Yum!

Web of Influence

Corporations wield tremendous influence in the global political economy. The power of corporations lies in its ability to influence and direct policy, research, finance, and trade in ways that will ensure its business the greatest economic returns. To grasp the far-reaching impact of corporate influence, we would like to map the relationships that these businesses strategically develop across all sectors of society, including:

  • Government:
    •          Agencies (e.g. USAID)
             Policies/Laws
                  Foreign (e.g. PL480, Export Enhancement Program)
                  Domestic (e.g. Farm Bill)
  • Political parties
  • Lobbies and special interest groups e.g. US Wheat Associates
  • Finance--hedge funds, investment firms
  • Academic--which universities and research institutes they fund
  • Trade agreements—how have corporations been involved and influenced trade agreement (multilateral and bilateral)

We should be able to display an interactive diagram that will connect each corporation to the above-listed actors to show the nature of the relationship and amount of money exchanged between them e.g. Cargill’s chief executive, Whitney MacMillan, was assigned to a GATT advisory group to help congressional leaders monitor the final phase of GATT negotiations OR Monsanto contributed more than $8 million to help defeat the GMO labeling campaign, Prop 37, in California in 2012.

Health

Hunger Malnutrition Gender Impact Environment

This section will examine the health impact of the global food system with relation to hunger, malnutrition and poor nutrition, gender, and the environment. Given the topics we will explore, it is important to note that health is not limited to human health, but also the health of the environment and how the poor treatment of the environment has led to a number of phenomena including climate change, dead zones, depleting freshwater resources, etc.
